1.1: Describing the purpose of IT in modern business.

IT serves many purposes: Data tracking, such as customer and supplier information, stock monitoring, order history, delivery tracking etc. Other types of data may also be tracked, how many times a user has visited a website, record any support tasks that have been carried out to help a customer are just a few examples.

Websites: If you want your business to remain competitive today, you NEED a website. It may be a simple one that's used to describe your business and give contact information, or it may be more complex and feature an online shop.

Advertising, with promotional materials often created in software like photoshop, then hosted on the company servers along with the website or by an external ad hosting company.

System Automation. Any sort of automation would be impossible without IT. Controlling robotic manufacturing systems such as those found in car factories, driving robots around a warehouse like Amazon uses, cleaning robots such as the roomba, safety systems in cars such as ABS, emergency stop assistance, and autopilot in planes (many of which were pure science fiction just a few years ago!) everything is controlled by computers.

As a customer front, allowing customers to buy products through the company website from the comfort of their own home.

Connecting companies with customers/clients for support tasks and other services. This can be done in a variety of way, from having an integrated live chat on the website, to simply listing contact details such as email and phone numbers so that customers can reach you.

Content creation, art work, photo editing, video and music production, and yes even websites... They're all done on a computer.

Accounting tasks, such as staff hours monitoring and wage handling, or payments and invoicing.

Security tasks, for example logging entrance/exits and tying it to time stamped video feeds or taking a photo for each instance.

Used properly IT systems should improve workflow, streamline productivity, and even reduce costs after the initial outlay. Sadly improperly implemented IT systems are apparent in every day life, company systems that require you to manually log everything you’ve done, government systems that require multiple inputs of the same information over and over again, other government systems that don’t communicate with existing systems and get information wrong.

1.4: Describe the risks that might impact digital projects - what causes digital projects to fail? Why?

While everybody knows about external forces such as government agencies around the world, hacker collectives/digital protesters, criminal enterprises, malware infestations and so on, these are actually much less of a threat than modern media would have you believe.

Bigger threats to your project are creator apathy, staff despondency, lack of funding/resources, failure in advertising, and hardware failure. There’s a reason regular backups are considered good practice!

The absolute weakest link that presents a risk, in any IT system is always the end user. Use of unauthorised and possibly compromised devices, visiting dodgy websites, falling for phishing scams and so on.

Other problems could be caused by not having enough time to implement all the features you want/trying to implement too many features in the time available. 1.7: Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?

Any information that is personally identifiable to a person is subject to GDPR:

Under GDPR, methods digital marketers use to obtain consent will be held to a much higher standard. Changes include:

- Active opt-in – Consent will require a positive opt-in through clear affirmative action. Pre-ticked boxes, for example, will no longer be compliant
- Granular – Individuals should get as much detail as possible on what their data will be processed for. Best practice will be to let individuals withdraw consent for each distinct processing activity. Blanket consent will not be compliant
- Unbundling – Consent requests must be separated from terms and conditions
- Not a pre-condition of service – Consent should not be a precondition of signing up to a service unless necessary such as a newspaper subscription
- Named – It should be clear to the individual who will be processing their data including any third-parties

Under GDPR a person can request copies of any and all information a company may hold on them, which must be supplied within 30 days. They can also request that all the data is deleted so that it can’t be accessed any longer.

GDPR only applies in European countries, as it’s an EU directive.

Anyone that operates a business, or even private technology that holds the data of others is subject to GDPR. This even includes things like dash cam and home cctv footage.

3.2: Describe ways to improve productivity and efficiency

Remove most distractions (though remember a bit of background music often helps improve workflow/concentration). Make sure team members take regular short breaks, but don’t have everyone take their break at the same time. Also make sure each team member knows their assigned task, and that they’re capable of completing it. Give incentives to your team, this could be bonus pay for completing tasks early, being allowed to leave early, playing games between tasks, prizes for the best/quickest workers etc. Remove obstacles to your workflow, ensure effective communication between team members, and make sure everybody has everything they need to complete the task at hand before they start.

3.4: Describe how you would go about testing digital solutions

Alpha and beta testing stages could be implemented. During the Alpha stage when the code is basically complete I’d use in house testing, mainly by getting the team that developed the code to use it, and try to find problems. For example on a website I’d assign a page or pages to different team members, have them test every aspect of the given page as thoroughly as they can; making sure all the links work, and point to the correct places, making sure all images appear where and as they should, making sure text is clearly legible etc. Then I’d repeat the test but swap the pages being tested between team members in case anyone missed anything.

Once all team members were happy that testing was complete to the best of their ability and all bugs had been found, I’d move on to the beta stage: Get outside testers in (someone from a different team, the end user/customer, or possibly even random members of the public) to have a look and see if they could find any problems.
